14.3.3 Executing a call instruction

The usual mechanism of performing an assembly language call instruction is this: 

     caller's code   callee's code       stack
      --- lo mem       --- lo mem         --- hi mem
       .                .                  .
       .                .                  .  caller stuff
       .                .                  .
      call FUNC        enter <-- FUNC      .   <--- %esp before
      after <-- ADDR    .                 ADDR <--- %esp after
       .                .                  .
       .               ret                 .   calleee stuff
       .                .                  .
      --- hi mem       --- hi mem         --- lo mem

when the caller executes a call instruction:

  1. The stack pointer register %esp is decremented by the size of a machine word.
  2. The memory address referencing the first byte of the instruction after is stored on the memory location referenced by the stack pointer register %esp.
  3. The execution flow jumps to the memory address FUNC, which must contain the first byte of the instruction enter.

The usual mechanism of performing an assembly language ret instruction is this: 

     caller's code   callee's code       stack
      --- lo mem       --- lo mem         --- hi mem
       .                .                  .
       .                .                  .  caller stuff
       .                .                  .
      call FUNC       enter <-- FUNC       .   <--- %esp after
      after <-- ADDR    .                 ADDR <--- %esp before
       .                .                  .
       .               ret                 .   calleee stuff
       .                .                  .
      --- hi mem       --- hi mem         --- lo mem

when the callee executes a ret instruction:

  1. The memory address referenced by the stack pointer register %esp is stored in then instruction pointer register.
  2. The stack pointer register %esp is incremented by the size of a machine word.
  3. The execution flow jumps to the memory address in the instruction pointer.

after the ret the stack is left in the same state it was at the moment the call was executed.

Now let's say that, when generating binary code for a call instruction, we put some static data right before the call: 

      caller's code              stack
         --- lo mem               --- hi mem
          .                        .
          .                        .
          .                        .
         jmp L0                    .
         data_machine_word_1       .   <--- %esp before call
         data_machine_word_2      ADDR <--- %esp after call
         data_machine_word_3       .
         call FUNC  <--- L0        .
         after      <--- ADDR      .
          .                        .
          .                        .
          .                        .
         --- hi mem               --- lo mem

let's name call_size the number of bytes occupied by the instruction call FUNC; when the callee begins execution:

  • It can access the return address by retrieving the machine word at %esp.
  • It can access the constant data_machine_word_1 by retrieving the machine word at: 
              %esp - call_size - 0 * wordsize
  • It can access the constant data_machine_word_2 by retrieving the machine word at: 
              %esp - call_size - 1 * wordsize
  • It can access the constant data_machine_word_3 by retrieving the machine word at: 
              %esp - call_size - 2 * wordsize
